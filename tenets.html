Our Tenets

These are not commandments. They are commitments — the practices we hold ourselves to as members of this church. Knowledge that is not acted upon is unfinished; these tenets are how we finish it.

  1. I. Knowledge is sacred.

    The pursuit of understanding — by ourselves, by others, by those not yet born — is the highest practice we know. To learn, to teach, to preserve, to share, and to create knowledge are the five sacred acts of this church.

  2. II. Proportion belief to evidence.

    We accept claims in proportion to the evidence offered for them, and no further. We make no appeals to gods, spirits, revelation, or any authority that asks us to stop asking. Faith — belief held in the absence of evidence or in defiance of it — is not a virtue here; it is the refusal to do the work that knowledge requires. We choose the harder honesty: to say "I do not yet know," to follow the evidence wherever it leads, and to change our minds when it changes course.

  3. III. We reject violence — in deed and in support.

    No truth is worth a wound. No idea is advanced by harm. We do not use force, threat, or intimidation, and we refuse to be silent when others do. We also refuse to fund, purchase from, work for, or lend our reputation to those who profit from violence. What we know to be cruel, we do not pay for.

  4. IV. We reject coercion.

    No one may be compelled into this church, and no one may be compelled to remain in it. We refuse manipulation, deception, social pressure, and exploitation as means of persuasion. We seek consent in all things.

  5. V. Your body is your own.

    The first knowledge any person possesses is the knowledge of their own body — what it feels, what it needs, what it can bear. That knowledge belongs to no priest, no state, no employer, no doctor, no spouse, and no stranger. We hold that every person has the right to gather information freely, weigh it by their own conscience, and decide for themselves what they put into their body, what is taken from it, and what is done to it. No institution may override that decision, and no community — including this one — may shame, coerce, or punish a person for reaching a conclusion we would not have reached. We do not require members to agree with one another's choices, only to defend one another's right to make them.

  6. VI. Learn, always.

    To live as a member of this church is to remain a student for life. We meet new information with curiosity rather than fear, and we revise our beliefs when the evidence asks us to.

  7. VII. Teach freely.

    What we have learned, we share. We mentor, we explain, we answer honestly, and we do not hoard understanding as a source of power over others.

  8. VIII. Preserve what is fragile.

    Books burn. Languages die. Memories fade. We protect, archive, and pass forward the knowledge that would otherwise be lost — especially the knowledge of those whose voices have been overlooked.

  9. IX. Defend knowledge itself.

    We stand against the burning of books, the silencing of teachers, the censoring of libraries, the corruption of records, and the deliberate spread of falsehood. We do not give our money, our labor, or our attention to institutions that distort, suppress, or destroy knowledge for power or profit. To harm knowledge is to harm everyone who would have inherited it.

  10. X. Share without gatekeeping.

    Knowledge belongs to everyone. We oppose artificial barriers — of cost, caste, credential, or culture — that keep people from learning what they wish to learn.

  11. XI. Create with care, and act on what you know.

    To make new knowledge is to take on responsibility for it. We work honestly, cite generously, correct our errors openly, and consider the consequences of what we bring into the world. And when our knowledge demands action — to speak up, to walk away, to withhold support, to help, to build, to repair — we act. Belief without practice is not yet faith; it is only opinion.
