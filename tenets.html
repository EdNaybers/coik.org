The Tenets

These are not beliefs to profess or doctrines to defend. They are commitments—ways of showing up in a world that does not improve on its own.

1. We commit to the pursuit of knowledge

We believe the world becomes better when people understand it more clearly. Knowledge—carefully gathered, openly shared, and honestly revised—is our most reliable tool for reducing suffering and expanding human possibility.

This commitment asks more of us than faith. It asks us to be curious when certainty would be easier, to change our minds when evidence demands it, and to admit what we do not yet know.

2. We reject faith as a substitute for understanding

Hope is not a plan. Prayer is not a mechanism. Good intentions do not reliably produce good outcomes.

When something is broken—socially, morally, or structurally—we believe the ethical response is to study it, understand it, and work to change it. Not to wait, not to wish, and not to defer responsibility to forces beyond human influence.

3. We commit to nonviolence, including non-coercion

Violence does not only mean physical harm. It also includes coercion, domination, and the use of power to force compliance rather than earn understanding.

We aim to persuade through reason, empathy, and shared inquiry. Influence should be justified, not imposed. A world built through force may function, but it does not flourish.

4. We accept human fallibility without surrendering responsibility

We are not perfect, and we do not expect perfection from one another. Progress comes from sustained effort, reflection, and correction—not from purity or moral grandstanding.

Membership is an intention, not a performance. What matters is the ongoing attempt to live these commitments honestly, even when we fall short.

5. We build community to practice these commitments together

Knowledge grows faster in conversation. Ethics strengthen through shared norms. Nonviolence becomes possible when people belong to something larger than themselves.

This church exists not to declare answers, but to host the work of figuring things out—together.

These tenets will not save the world on their own. But they point toward the only approaches that ever have: understanding reality as it is, caring about the consequences of our actions, and choosing methods that actually work.

If that sounds like something worth committing to—even imperfectly—you already belong here.
